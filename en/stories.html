Lab Stories

A lab is more than papers and data — it is a place where people grow together. Many of the stories below are drawn from the lab director's academic memoir "Resolving the Dilemma of Ethical Dilemmas: A Journey from Method to Theory" and the preface of "Behavioral Ethics."

A Decade of Inquiry: From Philosophy to Behavioral Ethics

Director Liu divides his ten years of moral dilemma research into three stages: scientific learning and exploration, methodology (the CAN algorithm), and the theoretical return to behavioral ethics. "Where these three paths converge is exactly where mental conflict lies."

— From "Resolving the Dilemma of Ethical Dilemmas"
“

An Insight at Shengshui Temple

After his master's, Liu taught at Southwest University of Science and Technology. The nearby Shengshui Temple drew countless visitors. He noticed their worship was often utilitarian — and discovered that when people feel a loss of control, they turn to worship, which ironically makes them more utilitarian. Published in Psychology of Religion and Spirituality.

— The first independent study
“

The CAN Algorithm: After "Colliding" with a Big Name

The inspiration for CAN came during a grad-school journal club. Years later, Liu found that international scholar Gawronski's CNI model shared his idea — both exciting and disheartening. But he found a breakthrough in parameter generation and proposed the CAN algorithm, published in Frontiers in Psychology after several rejections.

— The story of a method
“

Goodness and Good Action: Not Hypocrisy, but Balance

Why do people do things they know are wrong? The traditional answer is "moral hypocrisy." But Liu proposes a different explanation: when ethical principles conflict, people's goodness and good action may simply be executing different principles — not "fake" goodness, but a struggle to balance.

— From the preface of "Behavioral Ethics"
“

A Dialogue Across 500 Years with Wang Yangming

Five centuries ago, Wang Yangming advocated "unity of knowledge and action." In the afterword of "Behavioral Ethics," Liu writes that the field's pursuit — coordinating "goodness" with "good action" — is a dialogue across time with Yangming. It does not demand absolute unity, but seeks to understand the difference between knowing and doing.

— From the afterword of "Behavioral Ethics"
“

Three Mentors, One Path

Professor He Xiaoxing accepted Liu despite his weak psychology foundation. Professor Li Zhongquan set up a moral decision research group just for him. Professor Xiao Chengli taught him hands-on, guiding the shift "from philosophical speculation to scientific testing." "These three mentors were deeply kind to me."

— The story of mentorship
“

Cross-Cultural Lab: When Syed Came to Chengdu

Syed Zain Abbas came from afar to become an international master's student. Here, moral dilemmas are no longer a single-culture topic — different cultures' understanding of "what is right" becomes research material itself.

— An international perspective
“

Psychology in the Community

Research should not stop at the ivory tower. At the "Peaceful Sleep" community psychological service symposium, the director brought the lab's mental conflict research to community workers — and found resonance, not a gap, between academic language and real community needs.

— From lab to community
